    Student-Built Breadboards

    For the 2017-18 school year, our TSA chapter decided to compliment our newly implemented comptuer science curriculum through experimentation with a set of breadboards.

    Our starting aim was somewhat vague, as in we weren't exactly sure what we wanted to accomplishment. However, with a large portion of our TSA population composed of Physics students, we landed on the decision to program our breadboards to handle the stopping and starting of motors and other sensors on autonomous vehicles.

    Of course, we needed to start with the basics- learning mundane tasks such as lighting up bulbs or turning on and off a motor. Though as we continued to experiment over the course of 4-5 weeks, our knowledge of these digital componets continued to sharpen, until at last we felt comfortable with delving into actually hooking up a car.

    With some trial-and-error, alongside 2 weeks worth of build time, we put together a set of autonomous cars, which used line sensors to track when to stop, as well as a set of automated motors, signifiying to our cortex at what speed to drive.

    The team-oriented and collaboration-friendly nature of this challenge contrasted with our previous focus on a tournament-style project. In general, our consensus on this change is welcoming; it was refreshing to work together as a team again. We're hoping to apply our knowledge of this field area in a more heavily computer-science-oriented project for next year.



    Catapult Build Competition

    Having finished off our drone-building project, the team then reserved some time to reflect upon the successes and failures of their past collaboration experience and made the decision to dedicate each passing year to a new theme of technology.

    While we had delved deep into the field of digital electronics, a rising number of 'Principles of Engineering (POE)' students fueled the demand for a mechanical, tech creation.

    With that in mind, a wooden catapult seemed the perfect fit for our team. As a means to get the process started, we spent a few days researching various designs, skimming over their strengths and drawbacks.

    Armed with this preparation, we then sought out to construct models in CAD or through paper-sketching, before diving head-in to the hands-on construction. Using the plentiful resources of our technology labs, we blew through the build process within the span of roughly 2 weeks.

    Whereas with the last project, we worked collaboratively on a drone, in this instance, we structured the event more as a competition, where each student (or group of students) would design independent of the others.

    Under this framework, we held a tournament at lunch to celebrate the team's project, attracting students from all sorts of niches and inviting them to share lunch with us in the technology labs. Our final winners were Hailey ONeal, Ben Gokey, and Wiley Neumeyer, who proved their innovative spirit.



    Collaborative Drone Build

    As part of the 2014-15 school year, during which TSA was founded, club leaders arranged to build custom drones as a way to kick off the school's experimentation in areas of technology. Our aim was to teach club members that technology could extend beyond the classroom and engage with students on a new level.

    Over the course of 4 weeks, spanning from the start to the end of January, we used the 'DJI F450 Flame Wheel ARF' as a base kit, piling on additional technologies as we went along. The project gained especial popularity amongst Digital Electronics students, who tied their knowledge of electronical composition into the build.

    In the end, with a few weeks worth of work behind our backs, we waited for opportune weather to test out the drones, which are now resting, strung across our technology classrooms.

Team Biographies

The active involvement of our TSA chapter in school based and even community-wide volunteer/fundraising projects is routed in our officers' dedication to providing the club with opportunity for growth. We like to think of our club leaders as beacons for inspiration, which promote tech-involvement and the advantages in working collaboratively on projects.

As a chapter, we are also proud of the diversity that each member brings to the table. Representing students from all 4 grade levels, the TSA chapter boasts talent in an assortment of technical disciplines- ranging from aerospace engineering to computer science and even architecture studies.

Doherty High School founded in Colorado Springs, CO

Thomas B. Doherty High School was founded in fall the fall of 1975. Thomas Bernard Doherty served as our district superintendent from 1964-1977.

The school was established to quell the need for students in the Northeast portion of the district. As the newest, and most populous school in D11, Doherty High has earned its reputation across the city.

August, 1975

DHS Automotive Program Leads the Way

The first annual car show at DHS kicked off on in 1998, serving to rack in additional publicity to our numerous engineering and tech programs, with special focus on our renowned auto department.

Ever since the debut of the show, the tradition has proven well worth the cost of running, attracting spectators from all around the state.

September 28th, 1998

Makerbot 3D Printers Acquired

IED and POE classes were in high demand, and to meet students needs, we acquired 3 Makerbot 3D printers, aimed at helping our engineering students become more engaged, and better preparing them for STEM-related technology skills.

July 2014

Project Lead The Way Curriculum Integrated

Engineering students gained access to The Project Lead the Way Curriculum, which gave them more ways to access information about engineering and STEM careers in general. PLTW teaching methods were implemented in Doherty’s tech classrooms in the school year 14-15.

January 2015

VEX Robotics Club Brought to DHS

Carrying Over the robotic team management experience from a local middle school, Adam Truex (currently an IED, POE, and Technical Drawing teacher at DHS), put together a group of interested students and chartered a VEX Robotics team.

In years follow, the team had grown and competes annually in Arvada and Winchester, Colorado.

August 2015

'Future Business Leaders of America' Chapter Chartered

As part of the growing business department, and student's demands to practice their business skills in the 'real world', an FBLA club was chartered at DHS.

Ever since, students have been competing at district, state, and even national levels, all with the goal in mind to earn a name for themselves and network with other business proffesionals.

In addition to our Engineering, Architecture, and Automotive technical pathways, in May of 2016, students would finally have access to an updated set of courses on Computer Science: “Computer Science Essentials”, "Intro to Computer Science", and “AP Computer Science Principles”.

Future Plans
